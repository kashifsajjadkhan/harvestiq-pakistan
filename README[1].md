## 📌 The Problem This Solves

Pakistan loses **PKR 345 billion+** worth of food every year after harvest — before it even reaches markets or consumers. Smallholder farmers (who make up 90% of Pakistani agriculture) have no access to:

- Scientific guidance on how long their crop will last
- Real-time risk assessment based on their specific storage conditions
- Economic calculation of how much money they are losing
- Practical, crop-specific recommendations in their own language

**No tool like this existed for Pakistan. Until now.**

## 🧪 How to Use It

1. **Select your crop** — tap the crop you harvested
2. **Fill in conditions** — days since harvest, storage method, temperature, packaging, transport
3. **Enter quantity & price** — your kg harvested and local market price in PKR
4. **Click Analyze** — get your risk level, shelf life estimate, PKR loss calculation, and recommendations
5. **Share or print** — send to your extension officer, professor, or farming group

## 🔬 Research Methodology

This tool is grounded in peer-reviewed science and real field training — not guesswork.

**Primary Sources:**
- **CAAS Beijing Training (2025)** — Post-harvest physiology, ethylene management, cold chain protocols, MAP technology
- **Qianwei UAV Institute, China (2025)** — Precision agriculture, drone-based crop monitoring
- **Ahmad et al. (2021)** — Post-harvest losses in Pakistan: Quantification and economic impact
- **ADB (2019)** — Agricultural value chain assessment, South Asia
- **FAO Post-Harvest Loss Studies** — South Asian supply chain data
- **Kader, A.A. (2002)** — Post-harvest Technology of Horticultural Crops (UC Davis)

**Risk Model:** Weighted scoring across 8 variables — maturity stage, harvest method, days elapsed, storage type, packaging, temperature, humidity, and transport method. Coefficients calibrated against published loss percentages for each crop in Pakistani conditions.

> ⚠️ This tool is advisory. Always validate recommendations with your local Agriculture Extension Officer (Zila Zariat Officer).
